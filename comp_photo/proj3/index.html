This hybrid image is created by first taking a low-pass filter of Trump image and a high-pass filter of the Clinton image, then superimposing these two. When we see images close up, our eyes notice more high frequency images and Clinton appears. However, when we are far away from the image, our eyes do not notice the high frequency components of the image and we see Trump. Notice the distinct lines in the FFT for Clinton and the combined image. This is due to the alignment process creating a box around the image. It is also worth noting that I decided on good cutoff frequencies by taking off my glasses rather than viewing from a distance, so you may need to stand far back from the screen to see the low-frequency band.

Other Hybrid Images

  • Mario and Luigi

The difficulty in getting this picture to work was figuring out an alignment point. The difference in facial structures and head sizes means that aligning based on the eyes or positions on the outside of the head does not work. What I ended up doing was aligning based on the position of the nose, a prominent feature on the face of both Mario Bros.

This was my attempt at a hybrid image between Dan Garcia and Josh Hug. Unfortunately due to the differences in shape of head and direction of faces, the hybrid did not turn out very well and no alignment tricks that I could think of would save it.

Multiresolution Blending

Favorite Result

  • In honor of the Lunar Eclipse last night.

  • A normal full moon

  • A so-called "blood moon". Spooky!

Multiresolution blending works by feathering at different amounts across frequency bands. This is done because if the feathering is too low the edge between pictures looks too sharp, and if it is too large high frequency components stand out too much. By increasing the magnitude of feathering as we go deeper in the Laplacian stack, we can ensure that we are blurring the seam as much as we can without letting high frequency components through.

Bells and Whistles

You may have noticed that these blends have been in color. That is because instead of converting the images to grayscale in MATLAB, I ran blending on the red, green, and blue channels separately and then combined them at the end.

Looking Back

By far the coolest part of this project to learn was the algorithm for multiresolution blending. It's so interesting to see a compromise like that in the frequency domain, especially in images. I am used to seeing work like this done with audio and it had never occurred to me that similar procedures could be done with images.
