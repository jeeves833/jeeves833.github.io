Fun with Frequencies CS194-26

This project centers around manipulating images via their frequency spectrum. The most common manipulation used here is the low-pass Gaussian filter, otherwise known as a Gaussian blur. Passing an image through this filter removes high frequency content, like edges. It is highly preferred among other low-pass filters because it doesn't leave any artifacts. In this project, I will be using this Gaussian blur filter in creative ways to analyze images and achieve a variety of different visual effects from sharpening an image to blending two images together.

Image Sharpening

The key observation to make regarding image sharpening is that when subtracting a blurred image from the original, only the high-frequency details of the image remain. Adding these details back to the original image makes them stand out a lot more. This is the procedure I used in the following example.

Hybrid Images

The premise of a hybrid image is that the image looks very different far away than it does close up. This is because at long distances, our eyes naturally blur the images they send to the brain (which is why you can't read a book at 50 meters). So combining the low frequency content of one image (blurring) with the high frequency content of another (the details), results in a picture that looks like one of two source images depending on how close or far away you are. Gaussian and Laplacian Stacks

A Gaussian stack is a series of images where each image is the result of applying a low-pass filter to the previous image. In the frequency domiain, it can be viewed as a series of shrinking disks. In layman's terms, it's a series of progressively blurrier images. The utility of generating these Gaussian stacks is that it allows us to perform some operations (like the alignments here) much faster.

If the Gaussian stack is a series of disks in frequency, then the Laplacian stack is a series of concentric rings. They are the difference between consecutive items of the Gaussian stack and divide up the frequency space into bands. Superimposing all the images in the Laplacian stack results in the original image. This is useful if you want to perform nonlinear operations that are defined differently across different frequency bands. In the following examples, I will notate items of the Gaussian stack as Gn and items of the Laplacian stack as Ln.

Multiresolution Blending

Let's say you wanted to stitch two different images together (the left half is one image and the right half is another) in a way that looks natural. You can't just superimpose the two halves together because that looks horribly unnatural. Ideally you can cross-fade the two images together, but that causes issues when details bleed (ghost) across the seam in a way that doesn't look good. What is one to do? The solution is multiresolution blending. By separating the image into frequency bands, and blending the images differently in each frequency band we avoid the ghosting issue while cross-fading the images as much as possible to make it look natural.

More Blends

This blend uses a cross-fading mask that is not just half-and-half. It fades in an oblong shape around the mouth on the wall.

Of course there is some level of artistry to picking out pictures that blend well. Ideally you get pictures that have similar shapes that can be blended together. You can't just put pictures together at random and hope that it works out.
